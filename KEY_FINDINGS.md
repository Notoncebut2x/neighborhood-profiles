# Key Findings — Baltimore NSA Profiles (2019–2023 ACS)

---

## 1. The income gap across Baltimore is extraordinary

The spread in median household income across Baltimore's neighborhoods is one of the starkest findings in the data.

- **Top end:** Guilford, Homeland, Blythewood, and Bellona-Gittings all report median household incomes at or near **$200,000** — the top-coded ceiling of the ACS income brackets.
- **Bottom end:** Pimlico Good Neighbors ($11,952), Pleasant View Gardens ($13,023), and Penn-Fallsway ($13,952) sit at the other extreme.

That's a **17-to-1 ratio** between the richest and poorest neighborhoods — within the same city. The distance between Guilford and Penn-Fallsway is roughly 4 miles.

---

## 2. Rent is rising everywhere, but not evenly

Rent increased across virtually every NSA in Baltimore between 2014–2018 and 2019–2023. But the scale of increases varies dramatically.

**Biggest rent increases:**
- **Woodbourne-McCabe:** +1,187% — from a very low base, likely reflecting a conversion of formerly subsidized units to market rate. Treat with caution given the small sample size.
- **Perkins Homes:** +485% — Perkins Homes is a public housing complex undergoing full demolition and redevelopment. The rising median reflects the displacement of very low-rent public housing with new market-rate units.
- **Otterbein:** +216% — a small waterfront neighborhood adjacent to the Inner Harbor, now commanding $1,910/month median rent.

More broadly, even neighborhoods with significant poverty are seeing rent climb. **Cherry Hill**, where 88% of residents rent and median income is $28,133, saw rents rise 39%. **Sandtown-Winchester** — one of Baltimore's highest-profile distressed neighborhoods — saw a 31% rent increase against an income base that barely moved.

---

## 3. Downtown is Baltimore's fastest-growing neighborhood — by a wide margin

**Downtown** added 2,580 residents between the two ACS windows, a **42% increase** in population. It is now 95.5% renters, 62.6% aged 18–34, and commands a median rent of $1,769/month.

Its median household income ($67,626) is moderate by city standards, consistent with a neighborhood of young single professionals. Income actually declined 7.2% in real terms — suggesting the incoming population earns less than the prior wave, or that more lower-income students and service workers moved in alongside the growth.

---

## 4. Poppleton tells a cautionary tale about redevelopment statistics

Poppleton shows a **122,701% population increase** — from near-zero to 3,511 residents. This is not organic neighborhood growth. Poppleton experienced large-scale demolition of rowhouses as part of a stalled and controversial redevelopment project that displaced long-time residents. The near-zero prior population reflects cleared land; the current population reflects partial new construction. The percentage figure is technically accurate and completely misleading as a measure of neighborhood health.

This is a reminder that extreme percentage changes almost always signal redevelopment activity, not normal population dynamics.

---

## 5. The vacancy crisis is concentrated in specific corridors

While Baltimore's overall vacancy rate is well-documented, the NSA data shows it is not evenly distributed:

- **Shipley Hill:** 57.5% of housing units are vacant
- **Dundalk Marine Terminal:** 54.5%
- **Lucille Park:** 52.4%
- **Midtown-Edmondson:** 51.9%
- **Easterwood:** 50.5%

These five neighborhoods — all with incomes between $21,000 and $33,000 — represent a concentrated band of disinvestment in West and Southwest Baltimore. High vacancy in low-income neighborhoods is a self-reinforcing problem: vacant properties depress surrounding values, reduce tax revenue, and accelerate further disinvestment.

By contrast, high-income neighborhoods in North Baltimore have vacancy rates below 5%.

---

## 6. Canton and Patterson Park are now among the most expensive neighborhoods in the city

Both neighborhoods have undergone well-documented gentrification, and the numbers reflect it:

- **Patterson Park:** median rent $2,498, median home value data unavailable but income at $172,017 — the highest of any non-north-Baltimore neighborhood
- **Canton:** median rent $2,257, median income $156,462, median home value not captured due to high owner-occupancy in upper brackets

For context, the city-wide median rent is roughly $1,100. Canton and Patterson Park renters pay more than double the city median.

---

## 7. Transit dependency is concentrated among the poorest residents

The neighborhoods with the highest public transit commute rates are also among the city's lowest-income:

- **Pleasant View Gardens:** 87.7% of workers commute by transit — the highest in the city — against a median household income of $13,023
- **Lucille Park, Towanda-Grantley, Coppin Heights:** all above 50% transit commute rates, all below $35,000 median income

This pattern reflects a fundamental tension in Baltimore's transportation geography: the residents most dependent on transit live in neighborhoods where transit service is often least reliable. It also means that any disruption to bus or MTA service disproportionately affects the city's most economically vulnerable communities.

---

## 8. Education and income track closely — with some notable exceptions

The correlation between educational attainment and income holds broadly across Baltimore's NSAs, but a few neighborhoods stand out:

**Sharp-Leadenhall** has 83% renters, a median rent of $2,292, and a median income of $92,554 — suggesting a high concentration of high-earning renters, likely tied to proximity to the University of Maryland Medical System and downtown employment.

**Brewers Hill** has a median rent of $2,338 but only 49% renters — suggesting significant owner-occupied appreciation alongside new high-end rental construction.

Meanwhile, several neighborhoods near colleges show very high 18–34 populations but moderate incomes — **Loyola/Notre Dame** (96% aged 18–34), **Johns Hopkins Homewood** (92% aged 18–34) — reflecting student populations who artificially suppress neighborhood income averages.

---

## 9. Real incomes fell in several neighborhoods despite nominal gains

After adjusting for inflation (2018 → 2023 dollars), a number of neighborhoods saw their median household income decline significantly:

- **Pimlico Good Neighbors:** -64% in real income — now the lowest-income NSA in the city at $11,952
- **Arlington:** -55%
- **Poppleton:** -52% (confounded by redevelopment displacement)
- **Millhill:** -50%

These are not neighborhoods where people got poorer because of bad choices — they reflect structural disinvestment, the loss of stable employment, and displacement of higher-income residents without replacement. Real income declines of 50%+ over five years in the same geography are a signal of serious economic distress.

---

## 10. North Baltimore's wealth concentration is entrenched

The cluster of neighborhoods along the northern edge of Baltimore City — Guilford, Homeland, Wyndhurst, Roland Park, Bellona-Gittings, Evergreen — consistently appear at the top of every positive metric:

- Median home values above $600,000
- Median incomes at or above $150,000
- Bachelor's degree attainment above 80%
- Vacancy rates near zero
- Renter rates below 15%

These neighborhoods were built with racially restrictive covenants in the early 20th century, and the wealth concentration that resulted has compounded for over a century. The data captures the outcome, not the history — but both matter for understanding what these numbers mean.
